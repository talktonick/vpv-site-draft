Verbal+Visual · Design System

The quiet system underneath the brand.

A design language for the parts of the site that need to recede so the reader can read. Brand moments stay loud; everything else gets disciplined typography, restrained color, and a single coherent rhythm.

00 / Overview

What this is.

This is the working source of truth for the V+V design system. It documents the tokens, components, and layout rules used across the site. It is intended for two audiences: the V+V team building production work against this system, and any agent or collaborator producing artifacts that should feel like V+V.

The other views in this sandbox demonstrate the system in action: Components Lab shows each component in isolation with variants; Case Study and Thought Leadership show how components compose into pages.

01 / Type

A scale, not a font menu.

Two families. Five weights total. Neue Haas Display does almost all the structural work — body, UI, headings, labels. Victor Serif is reserved for editorial italic moments: bylines, italicized words inside larger Neue Haas headlines, editorial display headlines on long-form thought leadership, and editorial pull quotes. It does not appear in body copy, navigation, marketing headlines, or general decoration.

06 / Page layout

Three widths, one outer frame.

Pages share the same outer frame: var(--s-9) top, var(--s-10) bottom, var(--s-6) horizontal padding. On mobile (under 800px) those reduce. Inside the frame, content sections pick one of three max-widths based on their job.

--col-narrow 608px

Inline ToC cards, narrow callouts, focused content blocks.

--col-medium 832px

Reading prose, contact form, editorial article hero, article body column.

--col-wide 1152px

Case study hero, engagement timeline, chapter player, metrics rows, CTA blocks, related strips, two-column layouts.

The rule, in plain words

Outer padding
Page wrapper has var(--s-6) horizontal padding (32px), var(--s-5) on mobile (24px). Content never touches the viewport edge.
Top-level stacking
Sections inside a page get var(--s-10) margin between them via > * + *, reducing to var(--s-8) on mobile.
Centering
Every content section uses margin-inline: auto within its max-width. This centers horizontally without resetting margin-top set by the parent's > * + * stacking rule. The outer wrapper handles edge buffer; the max-width handles measure.
07 / Why

What this system is fixing.

The starting problem

The current site uses the brand identity (logo, purple, cream) as a field rather than as punctuation. There's no quiet editorial layer between the loud brand moments, so the type works decoratively instead of as hierarchy, and the rhythm between sections never quite lands. This system is the quiet layer.

What it gets right

Type discipline. Two families, five weights. Neue Haas does the structural work. Victor Serif appears only in defined editorial moments — bylines, italic emphasis in headlines, editorial display headlines on long-form, and pull quotes. Body italic uses Neue Haas Italic, not Victor Serif.

Color discipline. Six brand colors, no invented tints. Variations are opacity-based on midnight. Cream is the editorial default; purple is the brand moment, not the field.

One spacing rhythm. A 4px base scale drives padding inside components, gaps between fields, and space between sections. The page reads as one composition, not many.

Buttons composed, not duplicated. A single .button class with four variants. Context-specific names like .form-submit and .cta-block__primary are semantic hooks that compose with the base, not duplicate styles.

Consistent active state. All interactive "this is the current item" states use aria-current="true". One convention, accessibility-correct.

What it's still figuring out

The system is documented but not yet packaged. Components live in one HTML file rather than as atomic exports. Tokens are embedded in a :root block rather than a portable CSS file. Both are deliberate for now — the sandbox is the working reference — but a later pass will extract the tokens and components into a structure that other artifacts can import from.

Introduction

For the last twenty years, designing a digital product meant designing a screen. The screen was the artifact, the surface, the deliverable. When the interface itself becomes generative — assembled in the moment instead of designed in advance — what's the work?

The honest answer is that most of the work hasn't changed. Brand strategy still matters. Information architecture still matters. Knowing what your customer is actually trying to do still matters more than any rendering technology.

The adaptive shift

The interface used to be a finished thing you handed to a developer. Now it's a set of conditions a system will resolve at runtime. That shifts the design work upstream, toward defining the rules, the voice, the brand modes the system can render.

It's less about pushing pixels and more about teaching a system to push them on your behalf. The agencies that figure this out first will look less like web shops and more like systems consultancies.

What it means in practice

Three things matter more now than they did before: brand modes the system can shift between, structured content the system can recombine, and rules that govern what a good version of the interface looks like in any given moment.

Brands that have invested in these layers are positioned to thrive. Brands that built their identity entirely in the rendered output are about to discover how much of their equity was locked inside a specific pixel arrangement.

The cost

The transition is expensive for the studios that built their reputation on a single beautiful artifact. It rewards the ones that thought of themselves as partners all along. The deliverable shifts from "a beautiful website" to "a brand that can hold its shape through any rendering medium."

That's a harder sell and a longer engagement. The agencies that thrive will be the ones who can hold both the strategy and the system in their hands at once.

01 · The brief

The Challenge

Faherty came to us with a healthy DTC business and a storefront that had been stitched together by three different agencies over five years. Every new campaign required engineering work. Every A/B test required a developer. The team's intuition about what to ship next was strong; the platform's ability to support that intuition was weak.

The brief looked like a redesign on paper. In our first working session, we reframed it as something more durable: build a foundation that would let the Faherty team move at the speed of their own thinking, then keep building on top of that foundation indefinitely.

That reframe is what made the engagement possible. Once Faherty agreed they were buying a long-term capability, not a one-time output, the project scope changed shape.

02 · The work

The Build

The Year 2 build focused on three things: a modular theme architecture that the Faherty team could compose without engineering, a content model that separated structure from presentation, and an experimentation system wired into the platform from day one.

The hardest engineering work was the module library. Every section of the storefront — hero, product grid, lookbook, editorial story — needed to be a real component, not a one-off template. The Faherty merchandising team needed to assemble a new collection page in an hour, not request one in a ticket.

We shipped 28 modules in Year 2. By Year 3, the team was building campaign pages without touching engineering tickets.

When AI becomes the interface, what's left for design?

Introduction

For the last twenty years, designing a digital product meant designing a screen. The screen was the artifact, the surface, the deliverable. Designers handed pixel-perfect comps to engineers, engineers built them, and the result lived at a stable URL for users to find.

Something is changing about that model. When the interface itself becomes generative — assembled in the moment rather than designed in advance — the deliverable shifts upstream. What gets handed off isn't a screen anymore. It's a set of conditions a system will resolve at runtime.

That sounds abstract. In practice it means designers spend more time defining rules and brand modes, less time pushing pixels. The interesting work moves to a layer above the rendered output.

The deliverable shifts from "a beautiful website" to "a brand that can hold its shape through any rendering medium." — On adaptive commerce

The adaptive shift

The interface used to be a finished thing. Now it's a set of conditions a system will resolve at runtime — based on who's asking, what they've done before, and what the brand needs to communicate in that moment. The same product page might render three different ways depending on context.

This isn't theoretical. We're already seeing it in product feeds that adapt to conversational queries, in storefronts that recompose based on first-party data, in checkout flows that personalize the entire path through the funnel. The shift isn't a single inflection point — it's a gradient that's been moving for years.

What's accelerating now is the rate at which the rendering layer is becoming intelligent. A storefront in 2026 can do things a storefront in 2022 couldn't dream of. That changes what design has to do.

What it means in practice

Three things matter more now than they did before: brand modes the system can shift between, structured content the system can recombine, and rules that govern what a good version of the interface looks like in any given moment.

Brands that have invested in these layers are positioned to thrive. Brands that built their identity entirely in the rendered output — the static homepage, the campaign microsite — are about to discover how much of their equity was locked inside a specific pixel arrangement.

The work shifts. Strategy work, brand work, content modeling, decision frameworks — these become more central. Pixel craft doesn't disappear, but it stops being the deliverable.

The cost

The transition is expensive for studios that built their reputation on a single beautiful artifact. It's hard to sell "a brand that can hold its shape across any rendering medium" — it's much easier to sell "a beautiful website." The first is more valuable; the second photographs better.

There's also a real cost on the client side. Building toward an adaptive system requires investing in content infrastructure, measurement frameworks, and brand systems that show their value over time rather than at launch. Most marketing budgets aren't structured for that timeline.

The agencies that thrive in this shift will be the ones who can hold both the strategy and the system in their hands at once. The ones who can sell the longer engagement honestly, without dressing it up as something quicker.

What this means for us

At Verbal+Visual, we've spent the last few years moving deliberately in this direction. Our Commerce+ program is built around long-term partnerships precisely because the work that compounds the most is the work that doesn't fit inside a single project scope.

We don't think this shift is finished. We don't even think it's halfway done. But we do think the brands that start investing in the adaptive layer now will look very different in five years from the ones that don't.
